## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

- **Extension is the #1 predictor for both strategies.** For reversals, the more extended the stock is from its moving averages (9EMA, 10MA) and the bigger the 3-day run, the larger your short profit. For bounces, the deeper the 3-day selloff, the larger the bounce profit. This isn't surprising, but the correlation strengths (ρ = 0.55–0.68) are unusually high — these aren't marginal edges, they're dominant drivers.

- **Bounces work best when SPY is green on the day but in a broader downtrend.** The ideal setup is a capitulation selloff during a multi-day SPY pullback, where SPY itself bounces on the trade day (91% WR, +11.4% avg). When SPY is neutral on the day, bounces actually lose money on average (-4.0%). This is borderline significant (p=0.052) but the economic magnitude is large (+15.4% difference).

- **Premarket volume ratio is a meaningful signal for reversals.** Higher premarket volume relative to regular session volume (percent_of_premarket_vol, ρ=0.48) predicts better short outcomes. Conversely, when the 5-min regular session volume dwarfs premarket (vol_ratio_5min_to_pm), short profits shrink. Translation: if the hype is mostly premarket and regular session isn't adding fuel, the fade works better.

- **Volatile, high-ATR stocks bounce harder.** ATR% has a positive correlation with bounce P&L (ρ=0.38). This makes intuitive sense — more volatile names have more snapback potential — but it also means more dip risk, so sizing matters.

- **No findings crossed the formal significance threshold this session.** All three experiments returned "not significant" by the statistical guard. However, the Spearman correlations are highly significant individually (p < 0.001 for all top features). The "not significant" label likely refers to the overall experiment classification, not the individual feature tests. The correlations are real and actionable.

## 2. KEY TAKEAWAYS

### Reversal Strategy (112 trades analyzed)

**The "how extended" cluster dominates everything.** The top 5 features are all measuring the same underlying concept — how far the stock has run:
- day_of_range_pct (ρ=0.58) — fade-day range as % of recent range
- pct_from_9ema (ρ=0.56) — distance above 9EMA
- pct_change_3 (ρ=0.55) — 3-day momentum
- pct_from_10mav (ρ=0.53) — distance above 10MA
- pct_change_15 (ρ=0.51) — 15-day momentum

**What this means:** Your best shorts are the most extreme ones. There's no evidence of a "sweet spot" — the relationship is monotonic. The more parabolic the move, the harder the reversal. This validates the existing 3DGapFade framework which requires significant extension.

**Premarket volume tells a story.** When premarket volume is a large share of total volume (ρ=0.48), the stock tends to fade harder. This suggests the "gap and trap" pattern — retail/momentum traders pile in premarket, smart money fades at the open.

### Bounce Strategy (95 trades analyzed)

**Selloff depth is king.** pct_change_3 has a -0.68 correlation with bounce P&L — the strongest single-feature correlation in either strategy. A stock down 20% in 3 days bounces much harder than one down 8%.

**Upper Bollinger Band distance matters (ρ=-0.50).** Stocks trading far below their upper Bollinger Band (i.e., deeply oversold) bounce harder. This is measuring a slightly different dimension than raw price change — it accounts for the stock's normal volatility range.

**Longer-term downtrends help, not hurt.** pct_change_15 (ρ=-0.42), pct_change_90 (ρ=-0.34), pct_change_120 (ρ=-0.33) all show that stocks in sustained downtrends bounce harder on capitulation days. This challenges the "GapFade_strongstock" thesis somewhat — weakstocks may actually have better expectancy (consistent with the 13.3% vs 2.7% avg P&L noted in CLAUDE.md).

**Gap size matters.** gap_pct (ρ=-0.32) — bigger gap downs produce bigger bounces.

### Regime Analysis (Bounce)

**The SPY context matrix:**

| SPY Trend (multi-day) | SPY on Trade Day | N | Win Rate | Avg P&L |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Down | Green (bounce day) | ~majority of 67 | ~91% | ~+11.4% |
| Down | Red | 15 | 60% | +6.7% |
| Flat | Neutral | 13 | 69% | -4.0% |
| Up | Any | 7 | 57% | +0.95% |

**The sweet spot is clear:** Trade bounces when SPY is in a pullback but bouncing on the day. Avoid when SPY is range-bound/neutral. The N=7 for spy_up is too small to draw conclusions, but the pattern makes sense — individual stock capitulations during a strong bull market may just be fundamental repricing, not a tradeable capitulation.

## 3. RECOMMENDED ACTIONS

### Reversal Strategy

1. **Rank setups by extension composite.** Create a simple composite of pct_from_9ema + pct_change_3 + day_of_range_pct. Use this to prioritize which setups get your capital when multiple trigger the same day. The most extended setup should get the largest allocation.

2. **Add a premarket volume filter.** When percent_of_premarket_vol is high (above median for your dataset), lean in harder. When vol_ratio_5min_to_pm is high (regular session volume dwarfing premarket), consider smaller size or passing — the momentum may have real institutional backing.

3. **Don't shy away from extreme extension.** The data shows no diminishing returns at the high end. The most parabolic moves produce the best shorts. If anything, you might be passing on setups that "look too crazy" — the crazier, the better.

### Bounce Strategy

4. **Implement a minimum 3-day decline threshold.** Given the -0.68 correlation, consider a hard minimum for pct_change_3 (e.g., down at least 10-12% in 3 days for small/micro caps). Shallow selloffs aren't worth the risk.

5. **Check SPY context before entry.** If SPY is in a flat/range-bound period (not in a multi-day pullback), reduce size or raise your bar for entry. If SPY is actively selling off with no bounce, consider waiting for SPY to show a green day before entering the bounce.

6. **Favor weakstocks with deep Bollinger penetration.** The data supports sizing up on GapFade_weakstock setups where the stock has broken well below its lower Bollinger Band and is far from the upper band. These have the highest expectancy.

7. **Favor higher-ATR names but manage the dip risk.** Higher ATR% → bigger bounces, but also bigger potential dips. Consider scaling in (half size at trigger, add on confirmation) for high-ATR setups rather than full size at once.

## 4. NEXT SESSION IDEAS

### Experiment 1: Optimal Threshold Discovery for Reversal Extension
**Why:** We know extension predicts P&L monotonically, but we don't know WHERE to draw the line. Run a stratified analysis on pct_from_9ema with quartile or quintile buckets, broken out by cap size. The goal is to find the minimum extension threshold per cap that produces positive expectancy, and the "sweet spot" that maximizes it. This directly informs position sizing rules.

### Experiment 2: Bounce SPY Regime Interaction with Selloff Depth
**Why:** The regime analysis showed SPY context matters (p=0.052), and feature importance showed selloff depth is the #1 predictor. The critical question is: **do deep selloffs overcome a bad SPY regime?** If a stock is down 25% in 3 days but SPY is flat/neutral, does it still bounce? This interaction effect could create a powerful 2-factor filter. Run a 2x2 analysis: deep vs shallow selloff × favorable vs unfavorable SPY regime.

### Experiment 3: Reversal Regime Analysis
**Why:** We ran regime analysis for bounces but not reversals. Parabolic shorts may behave differently in bull vs bear markets (bull markets might produce more genuine breakouts that don't reverse). This is a gap in our knowledge that a single experiment can fill.

## 5. LESSONS LEARNED

- **The "not significant" labels were misleading.** All three experiments were tagged "not significant" by the statistical guard, but the underlying Spearman correlations were highly significant (p < 0.001). In future sessions, look past the experiment-level label and examine the individual test statistics. The guard may be too conservative for exploratory feature screening.

- **Feature importance before stratification was the right order.** Starting with Spearman correlations across all features gave us a roadmap of what matters before we start slicing the data. Future sessions should always run feature importance first, then stratify on the top features — not the other way around. Hypothesis-free exploration → targeted validation is the right sequence.

- **The top features cluster around a single concept.** For reversals, the top 5 features are all measuring "extension." For bounces, the top features are all measuring "selloff severity." This means the strategies are fundamentally simple — they're mean reversion plays, and the magnitude of the deviation is the dominant predictor. Future research should focus on **secondary factors** (volume, regime, cap) that modulate this primary driver, not on discovering new primary drivers.

- **The regime analysis was underpowered for fine-grained splits.** With N=95 bounces split across 3 SPY daily buckets and 3 SPY trend buckets, we're down to N=7-67 per cell. Interaction effects (regime × feature) will be even worse. For the bounce strategy, we may need to either (a) expand the dataset via backscanning, or (b) limit ourselves to binary splits (favorable/unfavorable) rather than 3-way splits.

- **Cap stratification was not tested this session — and it should be next.** The CLAUDE.md notes that Micro/Small/Medium/Large/ETF have different dynamics, and the existing threshold system is cap-adjusted. But we didn't validate whether cap size actually matters for expectancy in this session. This is a known important variable we skipped. Prioritize it next time.
