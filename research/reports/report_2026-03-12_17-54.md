## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

- **No statistically significant predictors of P&L were found** for either strategy. Across 32 reversal features and 28 bounce features, zero passed significance thresholds via Spearman correlation or Random Forest importance. This is itself a finding — it means the current grading system (A/B/C/F) is already doing most of the heavy lifting.

- **The bounce A-grade baseline is already very strong**: 84.6% win rate, +9.21% avg P&L across 78 trades. The filter sweep's "best" combinations actually *degraded* performance (e.g., the top combo dropped win rate from 84.6% → 64.3% and avg P&L from +9.21% → +4.56%). These are exclusion filters finding the *worst* trades, not inclusion filters finding the *best*.

- **Bounce correlation signals are directionally interesting but not actionable yet**: upper_band_distance (r=0.41), pct_change_3 (r=0.33), pct_from_9ema (r=0.33) all correlate with P&L in the "deeper selloff = better bounce" direction. This confirms the strategy thesis but doesn't yet yield a tradeable edge above the existing checklist.

- **The reversal strategy was under-explored**: Only feature importance was run (no filter sweep, no cap stratification). With 112 trades and 32 features showing zero significance, the signal may be hiding within setup types or cap buckets.

- **Small sample sizes are the binding constraint**: The most promising filter combos all had N=8-14 trades. Nothing here survives multiple comparison correction. We need more data or narrower hypotheses.

## 2. KEY TAKEAWAYS

**"The grading system IS your edge."**
The fact that no individual feature predicts P&L beyond the grade means your A-grade selection criteria already capture the relevant signal. The grade is a composite — no single component dominates. This is actually good news: it means the checklist is well-calibrated and you're not leaving obvious money on the table from a filtering standpoint.

**"Deeper selloffs produce better bounces — but you already knew that."**
The correlation signals (upper_band_distance, pct_change_3, pct_from_9ema, pct_from_10mav, day_of_range_pct) all point in the same direction: more extreme selloff conditions → larger bounce P&L. This validates the strategy thesis but doesn't add a new filter — it says "take every A-grade setup and trust the intensity."

**"Beware the filter trap."**
The best filter combo (day_of_range_pct ≥ 2.5 + vol_ratio_5min_to_pm ≥ 1.0) shows +13.77% "improvement" but this is misleading — it identified 14 trades to AVOID (the ones that passed the filter had *worse* performance). Even that is unreliable at N=14. Don't add filters based on this session.

## 3. RECOMMENDED ACTIONS

1. **No changes to entry criteria.** The current A/B grading for both strategies should remain as-is. There's no evidence that adding or removing filters would improve expectancy.

2. **Consider position sizing by bounce intensity.** Since deeper selloff metrics correlate with P&L (even if not individually significant), the existing Bounce Intensity Score (0-100) could inform sizing: larger positions on higher-intensity setups. This doesn't change *which* trades you take, just *how much* you risk.

3. **Continue tracking all A and B grade setups.** The temptation after a null result is to over-optimize. Resist it. The 84.6% win rate at +9.21% avg is elite — protect it by not over-fitting.

4. **Log vol_ratio_5min_to_pm if not already tracked for reversals.** It showed up in bounce combos and may be worth monitoring across both strategies as the dataset grows.

## 4. NEXT SESSION IDEAS

1. **Reversal cap-stratified filter sweep.** This session only ran feature importance on reversals. The 112 trades should be split by cap (Micro/Small/Medium/Large/ETF) and by setup type (3DGapFade vs generic) to see if filters work *within* segments. The reversal strategy likely has more heterogeneity than bounce, so a one-size-fits-all analysis may mask segment-level signals.

2. **Bounce exit optimization analysis.** Instead of trying to improve *entry* (which is already strong), analyze *exit* behavior: what % of trades hit T1/T2/T3, what's the average give-back from peak P&L to close P&L, and does time-of-low or bounce-duration predict optimal exit timing? This is where the real expectancy improvement likely lives.

3. **Temporal stability check.** Split both datasets into halves (early vs. late) and compare win rates and avg P&L. If the strategy is degrading over time, that's more important than any filter. If it's stable or improving, that's strong confirmation to stay the course.

---

## 5. LESSONS LEARNED

- **Null results are informative, not wasted.** Finding zero significant features across 60 indicators tells us the current grading system is well-constructed. Future research should focus on *sizing and exits*, not entry refinement.

- **Feature importance is the wrong first experiment when the target is already filtered.** Running feature importance on A-grade-only trades (which are already pre-selected by those same features) creates a ceiling effect. Next time, start with the *full* dataset (all grades) to find what separates A from C/F, then validate on out-of-sample.

- **Filter sweeps on small datasets produce seductive but unreliable results.** The "+13.77% improvement" headline is exactly the kind of noise that leads to overfitting. With 78 baseline trades and combo filters producing N=14, we're deep in multiple-comparison territory. Need 200+ trades or pre-registered hypotheses.

- **Cap stratification should be done FIRST, not after.** Both strategies mix different cap dynamics. A Micro-cap reversal and an ETF reversal are fundamentally different trades. Future sessions should stratify by cap before running any analysis, even if it means some buckets are too small to test.

- **Exit analysis is the highest-ROI next step.** Entry is optimized (84.6% WR). The marginal improvement from better entries is small. But if the average bounce gives back 30-50% of peak unrealized P&L before close, exit optimization could add several percentage points to expectancy with zero change to the entry playbook.
